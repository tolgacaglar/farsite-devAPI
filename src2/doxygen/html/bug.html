Member CDT_JulianDate (int year, int month, int day, int hour, int minute, int second, int millisecond)
While all authors agree that the Julian Date starts at zero at 12:00 noon of 4713 B.C. January 1, the Duffett-Smith and Meeus algorithms actually yields a JD of 1.0 for this date and time! The function here correctly reproduces all the examples in their texts, but yield a JD of 1.0 for -4712 Jan 1 12:00.
Calendar Date Julian Date Source
1985 Feb 17 06:00:002,446,113.75(Duffett-Smith p 9)
1980 Jan 40 00:00:002,444,238.50(Duffett-Smith, p10)
1957 Oct 04 19:26:242,436,116.31(Meeus, p23)
0333 Jan 27 12:00:001,842,713.00(Meeus, p24)
-4712 Jan 01 12:00:001.00Should be 0.0!
Note the last example. While this certainly deviates from the formal definition of JD, I will continue to use this algorithm since I also use their other algorithms for Julian-to-calendar conversions and astronomical date derivations.
